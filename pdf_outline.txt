Department of Information Technology   Semester VI     2023 -27 Batch                                        Page 5 ABSTRACT  
Electric Vehicle (EV) batteries are the most critical and expensive component in modern 
electric mobility, yet no standardized, vendor -agnostic framework exists to monitor battery 
health, degradation, and lifecycle performance across diverse fleet configur ations. 
Although large volumes of battery data are generated by Battery Management Systems 
(BMS), CAN bus interfaces, and charging infrastructure, this data remains fragmented and 
underutilized, limiting actionable insights for OEMs, fleet operators, and e nd users. This 
project presents an Intelligent Framework for EV Battery Health Monitoring and Lifecycle 
Management, developed in collaboration with Magenta EV Solutions Pvt. Ltd., which 
bridges the gap between laboratory -grade battery research and real -world deployment 
conditions.  
The framework is organized into four interconnected analytical modules. The first module 
addresses Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction and knee -point detection, employing 
classical machine learning models (Ridge Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost) and d eep 
learning architectures (LSTM, GRU, CNN -LSTM) on structured charge cycle data. A 
piecewise linear MSE minimization approach is used to identify the knee point — the cycle 
at which irreversible accelerated capacity fade begins. Physical consistency is en forced via 
monotonicity constraints and SEI -layer noise removal. XGBoost achieved the best single -
model performance with test R² = 0.83 and RMSE = 11.2 cycles, while the weighted 
LSTM -GRU -XGBoost ensemble delivered robust generalization across unseen batte ry 
IDs. 
The second module implements a Behavior -Aware Battery Management System (BA -
BMS), which fuses real -time driver telemetry with electrochemical state estimation. Two 
composite indices are engineered: the Aggressiveness Index (AI), which quantifies driver 
behavior on a normalized 0 -to-1 scale, and the Battery Stress Index (BSI), which captures 
electrochemical stress from current spikes, temperature variation, and voltage fluctuations. 
LightGBM achieved the best performance among all evaluated models with R² = 0.2548, 
RMSE = 9.10, and MAE = 6.11.  
The third module focuses on real -world State of Health (SOH) estimation and Thermal 
Risk Classification using large -scale fleet telemetry exceeding 50 million records. A 
Hybrid CNN -LSTM architecture achieved the best SOH estimation with RMSE = 5.29%,

0.997.  
The fourth module provides unified EV battery analytics covering State of Charge (SOC), 
SOH, RUL, and Mileage per Charge estimation without data leakage. Tree -based ensemble 
models dominated all tasks: Random Forest achieved R² = 0.9973 for SOC, Extra Tree s 
achieved R² = 0.9990 for SOH, Random Forest achieved R² = 1.0000 for RUL, and 
XGBoost achieved R² = 0.9466 for Mileage estimation.  
The framework demonstrates that integrating physics -informed feature engineering, 
domain -expert behavioral indices, and ensemble machine learning delivers a scalable, 
interpretable, and practically deployable battery intelligence platform applicable across  
multiple EV classes, battery chemistries, and usage profiles.

CHAPTER 1 — INTRODUCTION  
1.1 Problem Definition  
The global transition toward electric mobility has positioned the lithium -ion battery pack 
as the single most critical and cost -intensive component in the electric vehicle ecosystem. 
A battery pack in a modern electric vehicle account  for a substantial fraction of the total 
vehicle cost and determines the usable range, operational reliability, and overall ownership 
experience. Despite this centrality, the current state of battery monitoring and lifecycle 
management is deeply fragmented , technically opaque, and largely incapable of delivering 
the kind of transparent, actionable intelligence that fleet operators, original equipment 
manufacturers, insurance companies, warranty departments, and end consumers actually 
require.  
At the heart of this problem lies a fundamental disconnect between data availability and 
data utility. Modern electric vehicles are instrumented with Battery Management Systems 
that continuously monitor cell -level voltage, pack -level current, temperature d istributions, 
and State of Charge. CAN bus interfaces transmit this telemetry in real time to onboard 
controllers. Charging systems log energy throughput, session durations, and thermal 
profiles. The result is a continuous, high -resolution stream of operat ional data generated 
across every trip, every charge cycle, and every parking event across the entire vehicle 
lifetime. Despite this richness, the data remains siloed within proprietary OEM systems, 
inconsistently formatted across vendors, and largely unanalysed  beyond simple threshold -
based alerting. No standardized framework exists to translate this raw operational data into 
reliable, vendor -agnostic estimates of battery State of Health, Remaining Useful Life, 
degradation velocity, or thermal risk.  
This creates a cascade of practical problems that affect every stakeholder in the electric 
vehicle value chain. Fleet operators managing large numbers of electric vehicles face 
unplanned downtime caused by battery failures they could not anticipate, becaus e their 
systems lacked the predictive intelligence to flag accelerating degradation before it became 
a service event. They also face suboptimal charging strategies because no system is 
integrating driving behavior data with battery state to recommend charg ing windows that 
would maximize cycle life. Warranty departments at OEMs face disputes over battery 
degradation claims they cannot resolve objectively, because there is no auditable, physics -

Department of Information Technology   Semester VI   2023 -27 Batch   Page 2 grounded record of how a specific battery aged under specific usage conditions. End 
consumers have no visibility into the real -world State of Health of the battery in a vehicle 
they are considering purchasing, creating asymmetric information that suppresse s the 
secondary market for electric vehicles. Insurers have no quantitative basis for pricing 
battery replacement risk into electric vehicle policies.  
The technical root of all these problems is the same: the absence of a unified, data -driven, 
physically consistent, vendor -agnostic battery intelligence platform that can ingest 
heterogeneous operational data, apply rigorous machine learning and deep learn ing 
methods, and produce reliable, interpretable estimates of battery condition across the full 
lifecycle.  
This project directly addresses this gap. The framework developed here was formulated in 
collaboration with Magenta EV Solutions Pvt. Ltd., a company operating real -world 
electric vehicle fleets and managing battery assets at scale. The problem definition was 
therefore not constructed in isolation from real operational constraints, but emerged from 
direct engagement with the data, infrastructure, and business requirements of a practicing 
industry partner. The framework integrates BMS data, CAN bus telemetry , driver 
behaviour  records, charging logs, and trip data into a coherent analytical pipeline organized 
into four interconnected machine learning modules, each targeting a distinct aspect of 
battery lifecycle intelligence.  
1.2 Motivation  
The motivation for this project arises from two converging realities: the accelerating 
adoption of electric vehicles globally and the inadequacy of existing battery monitoring 
technologies to meet the demands this adoption places on safety, reliability, an d economic 
transparency.  
Electric vehicle adoption is growing rapidly across passenger, commercial, and fleet 
categories. This growth is driven by declining battery costs, increasing energy density, 
expanding charging infrastructure, and tightening emissions regulations. However, the 
growth also amplifies the consequences of inadequate battery lifecycle management. As 
fleet sizes grow, the probability of undetected battery degradation causing service failures 
grows with them. As secondary markets for electric vehicles develop, the absence of 
auditable battery health records becomes an increasingly severe economic inefficiency. As 
battery packs age into their second and third years of operation, the divergence between 
---PAGE BREAK---
Department of Information Technology   Semester VI   2023 -27 Batch   Page 3 laboratory -measured degradation curves and actual field degradation — driven by driver 
behaviour , thermal history, and charging patterns — becomes increasingly consequential.  
The scientific motivation is equally compelling. The lithium -ion battery is an 
electrochemical system of considerable complexity. Its degradation is governed by multiple 
interacting mechanisms including lithium plating, solid electrolyte interphase growth,  
particle cracking, active material dissolution, and electrolyte decomposition. Each 
mechanism has a different dependency on temperature, current rate, State of Charge 
window, and cycle count. Laboratory datasets collected under controlled cycling conditio ns 
at fixed temperatures and charge rates provide useful benchmarks but do not capture the 
stochasticity of real -world operation. Building machine learning models on laboratory data 
and expecting them to generalize to field deployment is a systematic error  that the research 
literature has only recently begun to address seriously. This project is explicitly motivated 
by the goal of bridging this reality gap using real -world fleet telemetry from Magenta's 
operational vehicles.  
The engineering motivation comes from the recognition that the individual machine 
learning techniques required — gradient boosting, recurrent neural networks, convolutional 
neural networks, ensemble methods — are individually well -established, but their 
integration into a cohesive, physically consistent, end -to-end battery intelligence platform 
applicable to heterogeneous real -world data has not been demonstrated at this scale and 
specificity. The challenge is not any single modelling  step but the full pipe line: data fusion 
across heterogeneous sources, physics -informed feature engineering, physically consistent 
prediction constraints, robust generalization across unseen battery IDs and chassis 
configurations, and interpretable output that can guide real ope rational decisions.  
1.3 Scope  
The scope of this project encompasses four analytical modules, each addressing a distinct 
dimension of EV battery lifecycle management.  
The first module addresses Remaining Useful Life prediction and knee -point detection. 
Scope includes the development of a preprocessing pipeline for charge cycle data that 
removes Solid Electrolyte Interphase noise from early cycles, constructs domain -informed 
temporal features including capacity proxy, rolling slope, and degradation rate, applies 
piecewise linear optimization to identify the knee point of degradation, trains and evaluates 
multiple classical machine learning models and deep learning archite ctures on chassis -
---PAGE BREAK---
Department of Information Technology   Semester VI   2023 -27 Batch   Page 4 stratified train -test splits, and interprets model outputs using gain -based feature importance. 
The scope explicitly includes enforcement of physical monotonicity constraints to prevent 
electrochemically impossible capacity increase predictions.  
The second module addresses driving behaviour  analysis and its impact on battery 
degradation through the Behaviour -Aware Battery Management System. Scope includes 
engineering the Aggressiveness Index from raw driver telemetry features including harsh 
braking, harsh acceleration, speed variability, kinetic intensity, and over speeding  
frequency, engineering the Battery Stress Index from electrochemical stress indicators 
including temperature variation, current spikes, voltage fluctuations, and State of Charg e 
drain rate, training and evaluating a comprehensive suite of classical and deep learning 
models on behavioural  and physical feature combinations, and interpreting results through 
correlation heatmaps, SHAP values, and gain -based importance.  
The third module addresses real -world State of Health estimation and thermal safety 
classification at fleet scale. Scope includes processing over fifty million records of fleet 
telemetry, building a sequential data pipeline for LSTM, CNN, and Hybrid CNN -LSTM 
architectures with temporal windowing and normalization, and building a thermal risk 
classification system using multi -zone temperature sensing across battery, controller, and 
motor temperature channels.  
The fourth module addresses comprehensive EV battery analytics covering State of Charge, 
State of Health, Remaining Useful Life, and Mileage per Charge estimation in a data -
leakage -free pipeline. Scope includes benchmarking a full suite of tree -based, line ar, and 
deep learning models across all four prediction tasks and identifying the best -performing 
model per task.  
The scope explicitly excludes hardware BMS development, OEM -specific embedded 
system integration, CAN bus firmware development, and any proprietary modifications to 
Magenta's operational fleet software. The scope is bounded to the data science and machine 
learning layers of the battery intelligence stack.  
1.4 Salient Contributions  
The salient technical contributions of this project are as follows.  
First, a physics -informed feature engineering pipeline for RUL prediction that incorporates 
SEI-layer noise removal, capacity normalization to post -SEI baseline, rolling slope 
---PAGE BREAK---
Department of Information Technology   Semester VI   2023 -27 Batch   Page 5 computation for degradation velocity encoding, and finite -difference degradation impulse 
signals. This pipeline directly encodes electrochemical domain knowledge into model 
inputs.  
Second, a globally optimized piecewise linear knee -point detection algorithm that 
partitions the capacity degradation curve into early and late phases by minimizing 
composite MSE across all possible split cycles, providing an analytically grounded 
identifi cation of the onset of accelerated irreversible degradation.  
Third, the behaviour -Aware BMS framework, which introduces two novel composite 
indices — the Aggressiveness Index and the Battery Stress Index — that operationalize the 
human behavioural  dimension of battery degradation, a factor absent from conventional 
BMS architectures.  
Fourth, a large -scale real -world evaluation of battery health estimation and thermal safety 
classification using fleet telemetry exceeding fifty million records, demonstrating the 
applicability of machine learning methods to the noisy, seasonal, multi -city, driver -
dependent data characteristic of actual electric vehicle operations.  
Fifth, a comprehensive data -leakage -free benchmark of multiple model families across all 
four battery prediction tasks — SOC, SOH, RUL, and Mileage — establishing empirically 
grounded performance baselines for each task on real operational data.

the four analytical modules in detail, each covering its data prep rocessing pipeline, feature

analysis, and inference. Chapter 10 presents conclusions and directions for future work. 
References are listed at the end of the report.
